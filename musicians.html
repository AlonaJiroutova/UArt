Mykola Lysenko

22.03.1842, Hrynky, Poltava Governorate - 6.11.1912, Kyiv

A distinguished Ukrainian composer, pianist, choral conductor, folklorist, educator, and public figure, Mykola Lysenko holds a place in Ukrainian music comparable to that of Taras Shevchenko in Ukrainian literature.(Ukrainian Live Classic)

Mykola Lysenko was born into a respected and longstanding Cossack family. He showed a natural talent for music from an early age, even publishing his first composition, a polka, at the age of nine. However, Lysenko initially did not pursue a career in music, instead focusing on science. In 1859, he graduated from the gymnasium in Kharkiv, and that same year, he and Starytskyi enrolled in the faculty of natural sciences at Kharkiv University.

When the Lysenko family relocated to Kyiv, both Mykola and Starytskyi transferred to Kyiv University. There, Mykola defended his dissertation on the reproduction of filamentous algae and earned a candidate degree in natural sciences.

After completing his studies in Kyiv, Lysenko chose to pursue a professional career in music and began his formal education in the field. In 1867, he enrolled in the Leipzig Conservatory. During this time, he actively promoted Ukrainian culture in Europe by publishing two collections of folk songs, romances based on Shevchenko's poetry, and overtures. On December 28 of the same year, Lysenko gave a successful concert in Prague, where he showcased Ukrainian songs arranged for piano. Upon graduating from the conservatory, Mykola Lysenko was expected to have a thriving career as a pianist in Germany, but he ultimately decided to return to Ukraine.

In 1875, Mykola Lysenko traveled to Saint Petersburg to further refine his skills in the class of Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov. After completing his studies, he composed some of his finest operas. Despite being offered a position as a kapellmeister in a private opera and the opportunity to join the Imperial stage in russia, Lysenko declined and chose to return to Kyiv.

Lysenko had long envisioned creating a heroic opera based on Mykola Gogol's novel, but this idea solidified during archaeological excavations at the site of the former Zaporozhian Sich. The libretto for the opera was written by Starytskyi, and Lysenko spent 10 years working on Taras Bulba. Both Pyotr Tchaikovsky and Rimsky-Korsakov praised Lysenko's work and suggested staging it at the Imperial theater in Saint Petersburg, with the condition that the opera be translated into russian. Lysenko refused. Consequently, Taras Bulba was never performed during Lysenko's lifetime. The exact reason remains unclear, though Mykhailo Starytskyi speculated it was due to Lysenko's modesty. However, given the imperial government's stance on Ukrainian culture, it's likely that Lysenko was prohibited from doing so. He was even unable to publish the opera's orchestral score.

He composed ten operas, including "Christmas Night", "Natalka Poltavka", and "Taras Bulba" (a masterpiece of Ukrainian opera), as well as children’s operas like "Koza Dereza" and "Pan Kotskyi", and the operetta "Chornomortsi". Lysenko was deeply inspired by Shevchenko, leading him to create the unique work Music to the Kobzar, which includes around one hundred cantatas for choirs, ensembles, and solo voices. Additionally, he wrote numerous piano pieces (including two rhapsodies), choruses, and romances set to poems by Ivan Franko, Lesia Ukrayinka, Oleksandr Oles, Mykhaylo Starytskyi, Heinrich Heine, Adam Mickiewicz, and others.

Mykola Lysenko among the members of the Lysenko Kyiv Amateur Choir, 1888.

Lysenko's strong pro-Ukrainian stance, especially in his art, prevented him from achieving a distinguished career as a composer. Despite facing language restrictions and persecution from the tsarist government, his creative work continued to thrive. Throughout his life, Lysenko supported himself by teaching music.

Mykhailo Starytskyi remarked that while Lysenko's strong convictions and genuine talent garnered him admiration and respect from his homeland, they also led to material hardship and significant blows to his self-esteem. These challenges hindered his career advancement.

Mykola Lysenko planted a crucial foundation for Ukrainian classical music and culture. Despite facing centuries of anti-Ukrainian policies, his work has endured and is now celebrated both in Ukraine and internationally. Today, Lysenko's compositions are performed in philharmonic societies and theaters across Ukraine, including in Lviv, Kyiv, and Odesa, as well as in countries such as South Korea, Sweden, and Italy. (Slukh media, Ukrainian Live Classic)

Solomiya Krushelnytska

23.09.1872, Biliavyntsi, Galicia - 16.11.1952, Lviv

Her voice was meant to captivate millions, a siren’s call that resonated worldwide. From opera houses in Odesa to Ottawa, and Naples to New York, she performed for enthusiastic audiences mesmerized by her rich, resonant voice. Each performance was a heartfelt expression of romance and longing, as she filled the venues with her emotionally charged melodies. (Forgotten Galicia)

Priest Ambrose Krushelnytsky with his wife Theodora (seated in the center) and children. Behind them are Volodymyr and Solomiya. Seated, from left to right: Olena and her daughter, Maria, Emilia, Anna. The end of the 1890s.

Solomiya Krushelnytska's early life was far removed from the glamour and acclaim of the opera world. She was born in Biliavyntsi, a small village nestled in the Ukrainian countryside. Her family belonged to the lower Ukrainian nobility, with her father serving as a priest in the Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church. When she was five, her family relocated to a village near Ternopil, where Krushelnytska first appeared on stage at just eleven years old. Her extraordinary vocal talent quickly drew attention and acclaim.

Although an artistic career was deemed unsuitable for the daughter of a priest, Solomiya's father recognized her exceptional talent and used their last savings to send her to Lviv. There, she enrolled in the conservatory, studying both piano and vocal music. In 1893, she graduated with distinction and made a triumphant debut on April 15 at the Skarbek Theatre in Lviv, performing as Leonora in Donizetti's "La favorita." She also found acclaim in the role of Santuzza in "Cavalleria rusticana." A pivotal moment in her career came when the renowned Italian soprano Gemma Bellincioni visited Lviv that year. Impressed by Krushelnytska's talent, Bellincioni advised her to move to Italy, believing it was the key to achieving international fame.

With Bellincioni’s encouragement, Krushelnytska journeyed to Italy, where she advanced her opera education both academically and on stage in Milan. By the early 1900s, her career had reached dazzling heights. From 1900 to 1939, Solomiya Krushelnytska achieved extraordinary artistic success, performing 63 roles in eight different languages. She graced the stages of major opera houses across northern Africa, North America, South America, and Europe. In the early 1920s, she shifted her focus from opera to embark on a highly successful concert career.

In February 1904, the renowned Italian composer Giacomo Puccini unveiled "Madama Butterfly" at La Scala, but the premiere was a dismal failure, with the capricious Italian audience reacting with scorn and boos. Puccini turned to Solomiya Krushelnytska for help, and she agreed to take on the challenge. Although she suggested that Puccini shorten the opening aria, which she believed was tiresome for the audience, she took the risk. In May, the revised version premiered at the Grande Theatre in Brescia, achieving remarkable success. Puccini and Krushelnytska were called back for encores seven times. Krushelnytska went on to perform the role of the famous geisha in major theaters worldwide over a hundred times and is still celebrated as the finest interpreter of Cio-Cio-San.

Solomiya Krushelnytska as Cio-Cio-San in Puccini’s Madame Butterfly

In Italy and Poland, Ukrainian singer Solomiya Krushelnytska was embraced as their own national treasure, yet she consistently emphasized her Ukrainian identity. She always concluded her solo concerts with Ukrainian folk songs, proudly showcasing her heritage. "Everyone knows my nationality, and I have never changed it, despite the fact that flattery and aligning with the stronger side might sometimes be beneficial," Krushelnytska stated. Despite her international stardom on the world's greatest opera stages, she made it a point to return each year to perform in her hometowns, including Ternopil, Lviv, Berezhany, Chernivtsi, and Zbarazh.

Her personal life also flourished when she married Cesare Riccioni, an Italian lawyer and the mayor of Via Reggio, the city where she resided during those years. Their home offered a picturesque view of the sparkling blue Mediterranean waters.

Solomiya Krushelnytska with her husband Cesare Riccioni

In August 1939, following her husband's death, Solomiya Krushelnytska traveled to Galycia to visit her relatives, bringing only two suitcases with her. However, with the onset of World War II, she was unable to return to Italy. The reasons for her staying behind during the German occupation remain unclear. While her friend Negrita Cano de Piacentini extended an invitation for her to come to Argentina, it was Solomiya's sister, Emiliya Sterniuk, and her family who ultimately accepted the offer.

During the Soviet occupation, Solomiya Krushelnytska’s home was nationalized, leaving her and her sister Hanna with only a top-floor apartment. To make a living during the German occupation, she offered private vocal lessons for a fee. After the war, Krushelnytska began teaching at the Lviv Conservatory but faced nearly being dismissed due to the disappearance of her higher education diploma. She was prohibited from returning to Italy, denied Soviet citizenship, and refused a pension until she surrendered her Tuscan villa to the Soviet state, which subsequently sold it. Despite her remarkable talent and voice, Krushelnytska’s life and career were constrained behind the "Iron Curtain."

Solomiya Krushelnytska gave her final concert in Lviv at the age of 77. Although her voice had lost some of its former strength, it remained clear as she performed "Rodimyi Kraiu" at the Lviv Philharmonic. She stayed involved in music until her death. Even as she battled throat cancer at 79, from April 8 to 16, 1951, she recorded four pieces, arrangements of Ukrainian songs by Andriy Shtoharenko, Stanislav Lyudkevych, Boris Lyatoshynsky, and Viktor Matyuk. Today, her legacy is honored with two memorial museums in Ukraine, one in Lviv and one in Bila, Ternopil region. Streets in Kyiv, Lviv, Ternopil, Rivne, Zhytomyr, and Buchach bear her name. Additionally, in La Scala’s grand hall, among portraits of renowned Italians such as Roger Leoncavallo and Enrico Caruso, there is a relief sculpture dedicated to the great Ukrainian artist, Solomiya Krushelnytska. (Forgotten Galicia, The Odessa Journal)

Mykola Leontovych

13.13.1877, Monastyrok – 23.01.1921, Markivka, Podilia gubernia

Ukrainian composer, choir conductor, pianist, teacher, and collector of musical folklore, he is renowned for his choral arrangements of Ukrainian folk songs. His Christmas carol "Shchedryk" has been translated into numerous languages and is widely recognized in the English-speaking world as "Carol of the Bells."(Ukrainian Musical World)

Mykola Leontovych was born in the village of Monastyrok, Vinnytsia Oblast. His father, a village priest with proficiency in the cello, violin, and guitar, gave him his initial music education. Leontovych went to the district primary school in Sharhorod and then studied at the theological seminary in Kamianets-Podilskyi from 1892 to 1899. There, he explored Ukrainian music and began working on choral arrangements. After graduating, he did not become a priest but chose teaching as his profession.

For two decades, Mykola Leontovych taught voice and music in public and theological schools across Podillya and Donbas, including Chukiv, Vinnytsia, Tulchyn, and Hryshyne. He continued his musical education in St. Petersburg from 1903 to 1904, where he sang with the Court Choir and obtained qualifications as a church choir director. During the 1905 revolution, he formed a workers' choir that performed at rallies, drawing the attention of the authorities and prompting his return to Podillya. In Tulchyn, he taught music and singing at a diocesan women's school for the daughters of village priests. Starting in 1909, Leontovych studied composition with the renowned Boleslav Yavorskyi, whom he visited periodically in Moscow and Kyiv. It was under Yavorskyi's guidance that Leontovych created his famous arrangement of the folk song Shchedryk (also known as Carol of the Bells), which remains highly popular today.

Alongside his teaching career, Leontovych was deeply involved in musical and organizational activities. He founded and directed amateur choirs and orchestras, organized musical performances, and promoted choral arts. He composed numerous choral arrangements of Ukrainian folk songs, including Dudaryk (The Piper) and Mother Had Only One Daughter. In 1901 and 1903, he published two collections of Podillya folk song arrangements for unaccompanied choirs. In 1916, following an invitation from Kyrylo Stetsenko, Pylyp Kozytskyi, and Oleksandr Koshyts, he presented his arrangement of Shchedryk with full choir accompaniment in Kyiv, where it received widespread acclaim.

During the brief period of Ukrainian Independence in 1918-1919, Leontovych relocated from Tulchyn to Kyiv, where he became actively involved as a conductor and composer. His works were performed by both professional and amateur ensembles throughout Ukraine. His original composition, "Legend" by Mykola Voronoi, was particularly well-received at one concert. After the Bolsheviks took control, Leontovych worked with the music committee of the People's Commissariat of Education, taught at the Mykola Lysenko Music and Drama Institute, and collaborated with composer and conductor Hryhoriy Veryovka at the People's Conservatory. He also contributed to preschool education courses and organized several choir groups. Following Kyiv's capture by the Denikinites on August 31, 1919, who targeted Ukrainian intellectuals, he was forced to return to Tulchyn, where he established the first music school in the city.

Mykola Leontovych among the members of the First Ukrainian Choir in Kyiv / Vinnytsia Regional Museum of Local Lore

Between 1919 and 1920, Leontovych focused on his first significant symphonic project, the folk-fantasy opera "To the Little Mermaid Easter," inspired by Borys Grinchenko's fairy tale of the same name. In the autumn of 1920, a choir tour was organized in Tulchyn, led by Kyril Stetsenko, with Pavel Tychyna serving as the second conductor. During this tour, the choir performed several of Leontovych's compositions.

On the night of January 23, 1921, Mykola Leontovych was staying with his father in the village of Markivka, Ghaisinsky district. He was killed by Athanasius Hryshchenko, a Cheka agent (Soviet state security), who had requested to stay the night, claiming to be a Chekist combating banditry. The following morning, Hryshchenko robbed the house and murdered Leontovych. The identity of Leontovych's killer was not revealed until a report disclosing his name was made public in the 1990s. (Ukrainian Live Classic, Ukrainian Musical World)

Volodymyr Ivasiuk

04.03.1949 in Kitsman, Chernivtsi oblast, - 24/27.04.1979 in Briukhovychi forest near Lviv.

Volodymyr Ivasiuk was a brilliant composer and singer who made Ukrainian pop music famous around the world. He was a doctor by profession, but a violinist, pianist, cellist, guitarist, and songwriter by vocation, which he refused to write in russian. (Suspilne Kultura)

Volodymyr Ivasiuk was born in Bukovyna, in the village of Kitsman, to teachers Mykhailo and Sofia Ivasyuk. His father was fluent in six languages and wrote prose and poetry, including about the Holodomor. When he was young, Mykhailo was imprisoned in Soviet camps for trying to cross the Romanian-Soviet border because he wanted to go to university. Therefore, the family was "penciled in" by the KGB as "unreliable." From an early age, Volodymyr was drawn to music; at the age of three, his greatest pleasure was rehearsing in the teacher's choir, where his parents took him. At the age of five, he began to learn to play the violin.

Father Mykhailo, mother Sofia, Volodymyr and sister Halyna Ivasiuk.

Volodymyr Ivasiuk would have graduated from high school with a gold medal if not for an unfortunate incident: in the tenth grade, he and his friends were walking in a park, and someone threw a hat on a plaster bust of Lenin. When he tried to remove the loose bust, it fell off the pedestal and broke. Everyone was taken to the police, the "Volodymyr Ivasiuk case" was opened, and the question arose of his expulsion from the Komsomol and expulsion from school. Eventually, the scandal was hushed up, but he got Bs in his school certificate in Soviet history and social studies.

The same story prevented him from becoming a student at the Chernivtsi Medical Institute, despite his brilliant entrance exams. Someone reported the "case" and on August 31, 1966, instead of Ivasiuk's student card, he received accusations that he had "dishonestly entered the ranks of Soviet students" and an expulsion order. So instead of going to medical school, Ivasyuk, an excellent student, goes to work at the local Legmash plant as a stamper in the hardware shop. There he created his first ensemble, "Bukovynka," together with fellow factory workers. Together with "Bukovynka," Ivasiuk made his debut on Kyiv television, and later, the then-popular singer Lidia Vidash recorded the song "I'll Go to the Far Mountains" on Ukrainian Radio, which was inspired by Volodymyr's ascent to Hoverla with his friends. The song immediately caught on with listeners and began to be played frequently on the air.

Only a year later, after receiving a recommendation from the Legmash factory, Ivasiuk became a student at the medical institute. Volodymyr Ivasiuk had two degrees. After graduating from the Chernivtsi Medical University, he entered the Lviv Conservatory to study composition.

In fact, the performance on Chernivtsi's Theater Square became historic when Volodymyr Ivasiuk, together with Olena Kuznetsova, performed the song "Chervona Ruta" for the first time to the accompaniment of the Karpaty ensemble. The composition caused a sensation, the performance gathered a crowd of thousands on the square, and traffic stopped on the square and neighboring streets. The song "Chervona Ruta," which was broadcast live, was sung throughout Ukraine the next day. "Those whose youth was spent in the second half of the sixties," recalls singer Vasyl Zinkevych,"remember well how Volodymyr Ivasyuk's song "Chervona Ruta" flew out into the wide world like a spring whirlwind, like a swift bird.

Ivasiuk brings his first compositions to composer Levko Dutkivsky, which are performed by the "Smerichka" ensemble and become extremely popular. Volodymyr writes songs and music for his friends, pop singers Nazariy Yaremchuk and Vasyl Zinkevych, and they travel together in the Carpathians. The filming of the first Ukrainian musical "Chervona Ruta" in Yaremche brings the young artists great popularity. The main roles were played by Sofia Rotaru and Vasyl Zinkevych, and many of Ivasiuk's songs were performed in the movie.

Ivasiuk, together with Sofia Rotaru, released a giant record with twelve songs by Ivasyuk performed by Rotaru - this was an unheard-of success for such young artists! Moreover, Ivasiuk, as a student, was not entitled to such a record; only members of the USSR Composers' Union had this right. Nevertheless, during 1977-1978, it had as many as eight reissues. It was the world's first and only Ukrainian-language conceptual disc: the envelope design was thoughtfully designed, each subsequent song complemented the previous one and at the same time was an independent unit. Although many fans created romantic legends about them, the composer and the singer were connected only by their creative relationship.

Volodymyr Ivasiuk and Sofia Rotaru

Ivasyuk gained popularity in the United States, where his record was released. The artist received a fabulous fee and, at the same time, a conversation with the KGB. Ivasyuk was strongly advised to give the money to a certain "Peace Fund" that was engaged in "spreading socialism." The principled Ivasyuk replied that he would not finance "terrorist organizations." He kept silent only about the fact that he had not seen the American fee himself, because it was simply impossible to get it under the Iron Curtain. Later, he told his family and friends that the KGB was watching him.

"The authorities hoped that he, like everyone else, would pay tribute to Moscow. After all, he was Ukraine's favorite composer, and he could influence young people 'ideologically' like no other. But Volodymyr was uncompromising. He wanted to serve only the Ukrainian people. Time passed. Ivasyuk did not create anything to glorify the notorious "reunification." Instead, he began to write an opera from the Cossack era,".

"He was repeatedly asked why you write only Ukrainian. He said: "Because I am Ukrainian and I want to."

On April 24, 1979, Ivasyuk received a phone call in the afternoon, got dressed, went to the conservatory, and never returned. The day after he disappeared, his family raised the alarm, but, according to them, they were in no hurry to find Ivasyuk and refused to place an announcement on television about his disappearance.
On May 18, almost a month later, Volodymyr's body was found on the territory of a military unit in the Bryukhovychi forest: Ivasyuk was hanged by the belt of his coat.

Obituaries and condolences to Ivasyuk's family were banned from being published in newspapers. On the day of the funeral, Komsomol meetings were scheduled everywhere with mandatory attendance, and there were instructions threatening expulsion and dismissal of anyone who did not show up. But people could not be deterred. Thousands came to say goodbye to Ivasyuk.

"It was the first time people disobeyed the authorities. A kind of protest expressed by coming to the funeral. There were meetings in all organizations, Komsomol and trade union meetings, to prevent people from attending. It didn't work, so it looks like people were just protesting. I heard people say that, and I thought, why should you forbid me to go to the funeral? I'm going to go. And what will you do? If you do, we will fight. It was the first unauthorized rally before what happened later," says Krysa.

Ivasiuk's close friend, the poet Rostyslav Bratun, who was the first to speak over the coffin, lost his position as head of the Lviv organization of the Writers' Union and editor-in-chief of Zhovten magazine. Disgraced by the party structures, he remained unemployed for 17 years, until the end of his life. He was not published, not allowed to speak, and even attempted to be killed - hit in the head with brass knuckles near his mother's apartment in Kyiv.

Volodymyr Ivasiuk said: "We, Ukrainians, become nationalists from the cradle if our mothers sing us Ukrainian lullabies. That is why we are re-educated in concentration camps."
(Suspilne Kultura, Ukrainian Institute of National Memory)
